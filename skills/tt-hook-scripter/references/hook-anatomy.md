# Hook Anatomy: the three-layer open and the timing budget

The TikTok hook is not one thing. It is three things firing inside the first 1 to
3 seconds: the **spoken line**, the **on-screen text**, and the **opening
visual**. Each does a different job, and they have to agree on one promise.

## The three layers

### 1. The spoken line (sound-on promise)

What you say to camera before the viewer can swipe. Job: land the promise for the
~70% who have sound on. Rules:

- One breath. If it needs two, it is too long for the open.
- A contraction and a fragment beat a full grammatical sentence.
- Say the number out loud ("forty-seven minutes"), do not just flash it.
- Cut the throat-clear. The first word is the payoff or the tension.

### 2. The on-screen text (muted-first promise)

The text card in the first second. Job: carry the promise for the viewers who
start muted, which is a large share of every video's first impressions. Rules:

- 3 to 7 words. It gets read at scroll speed or not at all.
- Different words from the spoken line. Saying the same thing twice wastes the
  second; two angles on one promise widens who it catches.
- No em dashes, minimal punctuation. A line break beats a comma.

### 3. The opening visual (frame one)

What is on screen in the very first frame. Job: stop the thumb with motion,
result, or an out-of-place object. Rules:

- Show the result (T1), the tension (T8), or an interrupt (T2) immediately.
- No logo, no greeting card, no slow zoom-in. Dead air in frame one is the top
  completion killer.
- Movement helps. A static talking head with no text and no motion is the weakest
  possible open.

## The timing budget

| Beat | Time | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| Frame one | 0.0s | the visual hits (result / tension / interrupt) |
| Spoken line | 0.0-1.5s | the sound-on promise lands |
| On-screen text | 0.0-2.0s | the muted promise is readable |
| The turn | by 3.0s | a reason to keep watching is established |

If a reason to stay is not on screen by second three, the video is already
losing the viewers it will never get back.

## The loop-close (why the last frame matters as much as the first)

A rewatch counts almost like a fresh view, and a clean loop is the cheapest way
to push average watch time past 100%. Design the ending so the first frame lands
differently the second time:

- End on the line that recontextualizes the open ("..and that is why the first
  clip looked wrong").
- Cut the last frame so it butts cleanly against the first (a visual match cut).
- Do not end on "thanks for watching" or a logo. That kills the loop.

## Worked example

Idea: a productivity creator showing a note-taking system.

- **Formula:** T1 Cold-Open Result (goal: completion)
- **Frame one (visual):** the finished, color-coded note board already on screen
- **Spoken line:** "this replaced every to-do app i tried in 2025."
- **On-screen text:** "one note, zero apps"
- **The turn (by 3s):** "here is the whole system in 4 steps."
- **Loop-close:** end on "..and step one is the part everyone skips", which makes
  the viewer rewatch to catch step one.

Note the spoken line and the on-screen text say different things ("replaced every
app" vs "zero apps"), both pointing at one promise.
